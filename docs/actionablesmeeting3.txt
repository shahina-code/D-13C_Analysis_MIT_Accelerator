SECTION 2: THE BIG NEW FINDING (not on the original list, but changes it)
================================================================================

While trying to improve the SBD peak fit (Priority 4), we found that the
"5 MeV peak" we had been using as our 13C+d signal — 355 counts — is NOT a
real proton line. It's a DAQ artifact.

WHAT HAPPENED: the detector electronics sometimes see two 2.46 MeV D+D
protons arrive so close together in time that it counts them as if they were
one bigger, ~4.84 MeV event. This is called "pile-up." It looks like a real
peak, but it isn't one.

HOW WE KNOW:
  1. A real peak's count grows in step with the beam rate. A pile-up peak
     grows much faster, because it needs TWO protons to land close together.
     We checked this using all 54 runs, which had different beam rates —
     the "peak" clearly grows the pile-up way, not the real way.
  2. The energies add up almost exactly: 2 x 2.46 MeV = 4.91 MeV, very close
     to the 4.84 MeV we were seeing.
  3. The detector's own settings file confirms it: two protons arriving
     within about 2.5 millionths of a second of each other get merged into
     one reading, and our math says pile-up would need almost exactly that
     same 2.5-millionths-of-a-second window to explain the peak size. Two
     completely independent checks landed on the same number.

There IS a small, real signal nearby, around 5.24 MeV instead of 5.6 MeV
(shifted because a foil in front of the detector slows the protons down a
little — this also explains why the D+D protons show up at 2.46 MeV instead
of 3.0 MeV). That real signal has only about 56 counts, not 355.

WHY THIS MATTERS: our "SBD sees way less than expected from CR-39" problem
just got bigger, not smaller (previously ~2.5x apart, now ~15x apart with
the corrected count). But it also lines up with a separate finding: when we
checked whether the CR-39's "background" strip is actually background-free,
it isn't — the whole piece of plastic looks about equally busy everywhere,
front to back. That points at the CR-39 plastic itself being heavily fogged
with noise, which would explain both problems at once.

TO CONFIRM BEFORE THIS GOES IN THE PAPER: someone needs to check the actual
beamline drawing for what foil (if any) sits in front of the SBD, and get an
unexposed piece of the same CR-39 batch scanned as a blank, to see if it's
naturally this noisy.

meeting how the real fix should work: for every left-right position (x) on
